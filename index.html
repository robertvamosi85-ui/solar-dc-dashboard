How Much AI Can You Buy — and What Does It Cost?

Two 24/7 power configurations for the same 10 MW solar park. Click the (i) next to any line for the methodology behind it.
Two ways to run AI compute 24/7 on this park's solar output: Scenario B is fully off-grid — solar plus a battery sized to firm the park's own annual average output, with no grid connection at all. Scenario C blends solar, a smaller battery, and a grid connection to support a larger facility, paying for grid electricity during the hours solar and battery can't cover. Both are priced on the same basis: GPU servers at €35,000–55,000 per unit (2026 new-hardware pricing) and operating costs at an explicit €1,000 per GPU per year.

Power reality
Park annual average output (the firm number) ~1.37 MW
= 12,000 MWh ÷ 8,760 hours ≈ 1.37 MW — the park's energy spread evenly across every hour of the year, day and night. Useless to a computer needing power right now at 2am in January on its own, but exactly the number a battery can deliver continuously if it perfectly time-shifts daytime surplus into nighttime supply.
Battery size needed (real-world benchmark) 60–70 MWh
Crusoe Nevada: 63 MWh for 1 MW firm
Benchmarked directly against the one real operating example that matches this setup: Crusoe + Redwood Materials in Nevada, which uses 63 MWh of batteries (built from second-life EV battery packs) to firm 1 MW of continuous, off-grid AI compute from a 12 MW solar array. Scaled to this park's ~1.35–1.37 MW target, that lands at 60–70 MWh — not a theoretical derivation, but the proven real-world ratio scaled proportionally.
Hungarian winter adjustment needed +15–20% more storage
less sun than Nevada
+15–20% added on top of the Crusoe benchmark, because Hungary's winters have weaker, shorter sun than Nevada's — more storage buffer is needed to survive multi-day cloudy stretches in December/January without running the battery dry.

Data centre sizing
Data centre sized to park average output ~1.35 MW IT load
With a battery acting as a perfect time-shifter — storing midday surplus, releasing it at night — a data centre running continuously at the park's own energy average (1.35–1.37 MW) draws exactly what the park produces over a full year, no more, no less. Size it higher and the battery runs dry in winter; size it lower and real capacity goes unused.
Number of GPUs to buy ~950
= 1.35 MW (1,350 kW) ÷ 1.4 kW per GPU ≈ 950. Each GPU, once you add its share of cooling, networking and power overhead, draws about 1.4 kW continuously.
GPU utilisation rate ~38%
marketplace spot assumption
Because this scenario runs 24/7, the data centre always has power — so utilisation here is purely a market-demand question, not a power constraint. On a spot marketplace, a given GPU is only actually rented roughly 38% of the time even though it's powered and available the rest of the time too.
Where you sell compute Spot marketplace or
direct AI company contract
Spot-marketplace rental (Vast.ai/RunPod-style platforms), or a direct multi-year contract with an AI company. A signed anchor customer at a fixed rate — rather than relying purely on spot pricing — is the single variable most likely to meaningfully improve this scenario's economics.

Plain-Language Verdict
B
Solar + Battery is the cleanest model — fully off-grid, no electricity bill, no DSO approval needed. At ~950 GPUs (1.35 MW), GPU capex of €33–52M dominates the €45–72M total. Annual profit of ~€1.45M/yr gives a payback of ~40.5 years on total capex — long, but the better of the two configurations modelled here.
C
Solar + Battery + Grid supports 50% more GPUs (~1,400 vs ~950) and higher gross revenue (~€3.5M/yr vs ~€2.4M/yr), but the ~€1.1M/yr grid electricity bill and the larger fleet's own running costs bring EBITDA back down to ~€1.0M/yr — essentially level with Scenario B despite the bigger build. Total capex (€55–89M) is higher than B's, so payback stretches to ~72 years.
Honest
Both configurations show payback well beyond a GPU's realistic 3–5 year competitive life — €1.0–1.45M/yr of annual profit doesn't come close to recovering €45–89M of capex inside any sensible hardware-replacement horizon. The variable that matters most going forward isn't battery vs. grid — it's the revenue assumption. Both scenarios rely on ~38% utilisation at spot-marketplace pricing; a signed anchor customer at a fixed multi-year rate, even at a modest premium to spot, is the single change most likely to bring payback into a defensible range.
Supporting Concepts — background for explaining the model
GPU replacement cycle — what's the real standard?
Two different numbers exist, answering different questions. Accounting/book life (what big clouds depreciate hardware over): Amazon, Microsoft, Google ~6 years; Meta ~5 years; CoreWeave 6 years. Real competitive life (when hardware stops being cost-competitive for frontier work): widely cited at 2–3 years, since Nvidia now ships a new architecture roughly every 1–2 years. Hyperscalers bridge the gap by cascading old GPUs down — frontier training years 0–2, inference years 2–4, batch/analytics years 4–6 — a cascade that needs scale and workload diversity a small operator competing on spot marketplaces won't have. A conservative planning assumption here would be 3–5 years, not 6.
What's the marketplace's cut?
Typically 15–20% on consumer/spot GPU marketplaces like Vast.ai. This commission is already netted out of the €2,500/GPU/yr revenue baseline used throughout this model — it's not a separate cost to add on top.
What does "Solar O&M" mean?
Operations & Maintenance for the solar park itself, separate from anything to do with the data centre: panel cleaning, inverter repair, vegetation/land upkeep, monitoring software, insurance, minor electrical fixes. Industry rule of thumb: ~€8 per installed kWp per year — for this 10,000 kWp (10 MW) park, that's roughly €80,000/year.
Can the battery be built from used EVs?
Real and proven, not theoretical: the Crusoe/Redwood Materials Nevada project (the benchmark behind Scenario B's battery sizing) uses 805 second-life EV battery packs assembled into a 63 MWh system — about 78 kWh per pack on average, roughly the size of a typical Tesla Model S/X pack. Scaling that ratio to a 60–70 MWh need implies roughly 770–900 packs. The open question for Hungary is sourcing — Redwood is a US company, and an equivalent EU/Hungarian second-life EV battery integrator hasn't been identified yet.
IaaS vs. MaaS — which business is this?
What this whole model evaluates — buy GPUs, rent raw compute by the hour, the customer runs their own software — is GPU Infrastructure-as-a-Service; a smaller independent provider doing this is industry-called a "neocloud" (CoreWeave, Lambda Labs, Crusoe). The alternative business — running an actual AI model yourself and selling finished output per token — is closer to "Model-as-a-Service" (MaaS) or an inference platform; there's no universally agreed acronym for it. Calling the token-selling model "IaaS" would confuse an engineer, since IaaS already means the GPU-rental model.
